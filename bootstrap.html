Hula

What is hula?

Hula is a dance accompanied by chant (oli) or song (mele). It originated in Hawai'i.

Any form of hula dance uses the entire body - facial expressions, arms, hands, legs, hips, etc. The whole body helps to tell stories and legends. There can also be instruments; the type instruments depends on the category of hula.

Hula Categories

There are two main categories or forms of hula: kahiko (tradidional) and 'auana (modern).

Kahiko was danced before any European/Western colonization or influence. It is accompanied by oli and traditional instrucments.

'Auana is considered the modern form of hula, as it is accompanied by mele and Western-influenced musical instruments such as an 'ukulele and guitar.

Tahitian

What is Tahitian dance?

Tahitian dance originated in Tahiti as an art movement of a civilization that depended solely on oral tradition. In ancient times, dances were directly linked with all aspects of life; dancers would dance for joy, to worship or pray to a god, to challenge an enemy, to seduce a mate, to greet guests at a ceremony, etc.

There are a variety of instrucments used in Tahitian dance, as well as many adornments for the dancers. The most notable of instruments are the drums - made of hollowed-out tree limbs and shark skin. Also, a pu (conch) is often used.

Tahitian Dance Types

There are a few types of Tahitian dance, including 'ote'a (traditional choreographed Tahitian dance), 'aparima, and 'ahuroa

The 'ote'a features coordinated movements, where dancers are arranged in columns and rows, accompanied by the drummers. Oftentimes elaborate costumes are worn, with flashy headpieces and big skirts.

'Aparima tells a story. It is a slower dance that emphasizes hand movements to tell such stories.

'Ahuroa is a slower version of an 'aparima. This type also tells a story through hand movements.

An 'ori is a solo dance. The dancer may or may not know the drumming, but the goal is to be able to listen to and easily transition between drum beats.
